Hints for exhibitors

  • ALWAYS take more exhibits to the Show than you will need, then choose the best just before you stage your entry.
  • Remember CONDITION AND UNIFORMITY. Every exhibit should be in its most perfect phase and uniform in shape, size and condition.
  • CHECK CAREFULLY that you have the correct number of items as required by the schedule.
  • DO ALLOW PLENTY OF TIME TO STAGE YOUR EXHIBITS. Avoid a last minute rush by completing your entry form at home.
  • EXHIBITORS’ adhesive numbers should be clearly visible on glass jar or bottle. They should NOT be fixed to the cling film, but on the edge of plate or dish.
  • Numbered stickers should not be stuck to fruit or vegetable entries, but stuck to a plate or card.
  • YOUNGER CHILDREN are welcome, and encouraged, to enter the classes for older children.
  • ALL CHILDREN’S entries are free but must pay if they enter any of the adult classes.

Flowers

  • The ideal time to cut flowers is in the evening (or early morning if that is not possible).
  • Flowers should be cut with as much stem as possible, making a slanting cut to assist the uptake of water. Sometimes flowers may not absorb water easily and the stems can be slit upwards (approximately 75mm) under water to assist.
  • After cutting, remove side shoots, unwanted buds and lower leaves and place the flowers upright in a container of clean deep water. It is better for this to be done overnight if possible, and then place the container of flowers in a cool dark room.
  • Flowers must be exhibited in water.
  • The flowers in a Posy in a jam-jar need to be tied.

Pot Plants

  • The ‘all-round effect’ is important in most pot plants, and plants should be turned every few days. This is particularly important in the few days before a show.
  • If necessary, flowers and leaves should be gently ‘teased’ out so that the plant is displayed at its best.
  • Final preparation should involve removing any faded foliage and flowers back to the base, and ensuring that the container is clean.
  • Stage the plant so that its best side is towards the front.
  • House plants should have been owned for 6 months.

Fruit

  • Pick as near to the show time as possible, taking care not to damage fruit.
  • Handle fruit as little and as gently as possible and by their stalks, if possible, so that the natural bloom is not spoilt.
  • Use scissors to remove soft fruits.
  • All fruits must be shown with stalks intact and ‘bloom’ left on the fruit. Under no circumstances should fruit be polished.
  • The stalks and calyces of raspberries should look green and fresh and all point one way. Reject malformed and damaged fruits.

Vegetables

  • Where necessary, vegetables should be carefully washed to remove soil, but in no circumstances should oils or similar substances be applied in an attempt to enhance their appearance.
  • Wash with a soft cloth and plenty of water: brushing will damage the skin and spoil the appearance of the exhibit.
  • Retain the natural ‘bloom’ wherever possible.

Beans (all kinds)

  • Exhibit fresh pods of uniform colour.
  • Stage a uniform-sized exhibit with pods arranged on a plate in a line with the tail ends facing the front.
  • Cut all pods from the vine with scissors, so that each pod has portion of stalk.

Beetroot

  • Select roots of even size.
  • Avoid specimens with poor skin colour at the base of the root or dat do not have a single small taproot.
  • Small side roots should be removed.
  • Take care in washing as all marks will show up clearly after a few hours.
  • Trim foliage to approximately 75mm.

Carrots

  • Choose firm, fresh, blemish-free specimens of good even colour and uniformity, without discolouration at the top.
  • Carrots should have the soil or growing medium soaked at the time of lifting to minimise damage to the root.
  • Cut off the foliage to approximately 75mm.
  • Lay side by side in a triangular formation with the root end facing the front.

Cucumbers

  • Fruits should be completely matched and of a good, fresh colour.
  • The flower end should be completely developed, the barrel well shaped and with a short handle.
  • It is not necessary that flowers remain attached.
  • Cucumbers grown under protection should be 250mm or more in length.
  • Outdoor-grown cucumbers can be smaller.

Courgettes

  • Select young, tender, shapely and uniform fruits approximately 150mm in length, and approximately 25-35mm in diameter, in any colour, or in the case of round cultivars approximately 75mm in diameter.
  • Stage flat with or without flowers still attached.

Garlic

  • Clean off all soil fragments, dry completely. Reduce the dried stem to approximately 25mm and remove the roots.
  • Stage bulbs as complete specimens; do not dived into segments (cloves).

Leeks

  • Specimens should be uniform in length, in good condition and solid (ie firm and compact throughput the length of the barrel) with a good, uniform blanch that is not bulbous at the base.
  • Avoid excessive stripping of outer leaves, otherwise unsightly ribbing is exposed.
  • Stage leeks flat on a plate with the roots to the front, neatly teased out and well cleaned.
  • Ensure that stem (barrel), leaves (flags) and roots (beard) are flushed clean with tapwater, which should not be allowed to run between the leaves leaving unsightly soil particles.

Onions

  • Uniform, well-ripened bulbs of good colour are required.
  • Avoid soft, stained specimens with thick, immature necks.
  • Do not over-skin.
  • The tops should be neatly tied and the roots neatly trimmed back to the basal plate.
  • They can be whipped with non-coloured raffia. Judges prefer well-dressed onions.

Peppers, sweet and hot(chilli)

  • Select fruit of the right shape, size and colour for the cultivar.
  • The exhibit should be uniform in colour.
  • Fruit may be shown immature but fully formed, usually green, or at the mature or coloured stage.
  • Mature specimens are to be preferred.

Potatoes

  • Select medium-sized specimens, generally between 200g and 250g, to fit in the palm of hand.
  • Choose equally matched tubers with shallow eyes.
  • Freedom from skin blemishes that may be caused by pests, disease or careless handling is important.
  • Very carefully wash the tubers in clean water with a soft sponge - do not use a coarse loth or brush.
  • Stage on a plate with the rose end outwards.

Rhubarb

  • Stalks should be fresh, straight, long and tender with well developed colouring.
  • Cut off top foliage of natural rhubarb leaving approximately 75mm from start of leaf stalks.
  • Do not cut off foliage of forced rhubarb.
  • Wipe stalks clean and trim off any bud scales at the bottom.
  • Rhubarb must be pulled.

Sweet corn

  • Cobs of uniform size with fresh green husks should be displayed with approximately one quarter of the grain exposed by pulling down sharply, from the tip to the base.
  • The best cobs are filled to the tip with straight rows of tender grains.
  • The grains should be well-filled, not shrivelled.
  • The stalks should be trimmed.

Tomatoes

  • Select fruit of the right shape, size and colour for the cultivar.
  • Fruit should not be overripe or with hard ‘green back’ colouring around the calyx.
  • Aim for a uniform firm set of ripe fruits with firm, fresh calyces. Stage on a plate, calyx uppermost.

Five vegetables in a seed tray

  • The vegetables should be trimmed to fit inside the tray.
  • All five vegetables need to be different and NOT belong to the same class of vegetables, e.g. a broad bean and a runner bean are both legumes.

Culinary

  • Jars and bottles must be clear alas with no commercial markings - lids also.
  • Jars should be clean, free from stickiness and well-polished.
  • All jars/bottles should be labeled with the contents and the date.
  • Labels should be neat & applied as near to the bottom of the jar as possible.
  • Plain scones are cut using a round non fluted cutter.
  • Tarts have no pastry on top but pies do.
  • Victoria Sponge is to be filled with raspberry jam with caster sugar (optional) on top, but not icing sugar.

Bottle Classes

  • Wine must be shown in clear, shouldered bottles fitted with a flanged cork or screw-cap.
  • A small, simple label should show contents and year.

Most of these suggestions to exhibitors come from "The Horticultural Show Handbook", an official RHS guide to organising, judging and competing in a show.
